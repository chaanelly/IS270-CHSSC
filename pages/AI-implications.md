# AI Implications

| Issue  | Description  |
| ---  | ---  |
| Risk to Archival Jobs  | Some archivists are concerned that AI tools for automated metadata creation could change the profession (Potter, 2025). |
| Cultural Sensitivity  | Both interviewees noted that describing culturally specific collections often requires contextual knowledge that AI systems cannot easily replicate. AI use can conflict with “cultural protocols or community norms” (Lo, 2026).  |
| Non-Consensual Use  | During the conversation with David, he shared that the organization has not had explicit conversations about AI, and that they need to create AI guidelines. He shared concerns about putting digital archives online because there's the risk that people will put those records through AI. Generative AI needs training data to absorb information and once absorbed, it's virtually impossible to identify the original source item and therefore define provenance. Gen AI should not be allowed in the archive because, “information without origin is a liability” (Lo, 2026).  |
| Perpetuating Hegemonic Bias | People working within community archives are especially skeptical of AI working with materials from historically marginalized communities. Technology replicates the systems that created it, and if left unchecked, AI could “perpetuate skewed interpretations of history” leading to generations of repercussions and widening of archival gaps (Johnson, 2025).  |
| “Human-centered Approach”  | During our conversation with Riona she expressed real ethical concerns about the use of any AI, especially generative AI. She refuses to use it, although does admit it could support transcribing–although a human would still need to check the work. That thought is in line with Stanford’s AI Literacy guide, that promotes a “human-centered approach,” advocating that all AI use is done to support human efforts and not as a replacement to them (Houston, 2026).  |
| Environmental Costs  | AI’s impact extends beyond the physical archive and repercussions for use may be hiding from the immediate sight. However: “AI systems require vast amounts of energy and water to train and maintain. The environmental impact is obscured behind images of frictionless digital efficiency—but it’s something we need to account for in our decision-making.” (Houston, 2026)  |
